\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

\subsection{Background}

With the rapid deployment of Internet of Things (IoT) sensing networks in smart city infrastructures, large volumes of environmental observations such as rainfall, temperature, and humidity can be continuously collected by geographically distributed sensors \cite{GUBBI20131645}\cite{6740844}. In addition, historical meteorological records obtained from public data services such as the Open-Meteo Historical Weather API further enrich the available weather data. These data sources provide important resources for data-driven urban rainfall prediction and environmental monitoring \cite{CHAN2023100375}\cite{10.3389/frwa.2024.1378598}. However, the distributed nature of IoT data makes traditional centralized machine learning approaches less suitable for real-world deployments, as raw sensor data must be frequently transmitted to cloud servers for model training \cite{pmlr-v54-mcmahan17a}. This process may introduce privacy concerns and generate substantial communication costs, particularly in bandwidth-constrained and unstable IoT networks \cite{pmlr-v54-mcmahan17a}\cite{2019arXiv191204977K}.


To address these limitations, distributed learning paradigms such as Federated Learning (FL) and Federated Split Learning (FSL) have been proposed \cite{pmlr-v54-mcmahan17a}. FL enables multiple clients to collaboratively train a shared model without directly sharing raw data \cite{pmlr-v54-mcmahan17a}, while FSL further reduces the computational burden on edge devices by splitting the neural network between clients and a central server. In this system, clients compute only the early layers of the model and transmit intermediate activations to the server, which completes the remaining training process. While this hybrid architecture improves both privacy preservation and computational efficiency, it also introduces new communication challenges in distributed IoT environments \cite{vepakomma2018splitlearninghealthdistributed}.


\subsection{Motivation}

However, communication overhead remains one of the major challenges in practical FSL deployments for real-world IoT scenarios \cite{vepakomma2018splitlearninghealthdistributed}. In traditional FL systems, communication mainly occurs through model parameter updates exchanged between clients and servers \cite{pmlr-v54-mcmahan17a}. In contrast, FSL requires the transmission of intermediate activations and gradients during each training step, which can significantly increase communication frequency and data volume \cite{vepakomma2018splitlearninghealthdistributed}.


In real-world IoT deployments, network conditions are often unstable, with issues such as variable latency, packet loss, or sensor failures. These factors further complicate the design of communication-efficient distributed learning systems \cite{2019arXiv191204977K}. While this work does not explicitly model packet loss or sensor failures, such practical constraints further highlight the importance of reducing communication overhead in distributed IoT learning systems \cite{2019arXiv191204977K}.


As neural networks become deeper and more complex, the size of transmitted activations can grow substantially \cite{vepakomma2018splitlearninghealthdistributed}. In addition, many existing systems treat synchronisation intervals as fixed parameters, resulting in frequent communication events that further increase network traffic.


These observations suggest that communication efficiency in FSL is influenced by two key factors: the size of transmitted activations and the frequency of synchronisation between clients and the server\cite{vepakomma2018splitlearninghealthdistributed}. However, these two dimensions are often studied independently in existing literature, and few studies systematically investigate their combined impact on communication cost, convergence behaviour, and model performance in real-world IoT environments \cite{vepakomma2018splitlearninghealthdistributed}.


Furthermore, rainfall prediction in real-world IoT environments presents additional challenges, including data imbalance caused by the rarity of heavy rainfall events and unstable network conditions among distributed sensors \cite{CHAN2023100375}\cite{10.3389/frwa.2024.1378598}. These factors further motivate the need for communication-efficient and robust distributed learning systems.
Although the proposed system is generally applicable to a wide range of IoT-based prediction tasks, such as air quality monitoring, traffic flow prediction, and environmental forecasting \cite{6740844}, this study focuses on rainfall prediction as a representative application scenario. Rainfall prediction plays an important role in urban management, flood prevention, and early warning systems, particularly in cities where extreme weather events may cause significant disruptions \cite{CHAN2023100375}.


In addition, rainfall datasets collected from distributed weather sensors provide a suitable testbed for evaluating communication-efficient distributed learning methods in practical IoT environments \cite{6740844}\cite{10.3389/frwa.2024.1378598}.


\subsection{Problem Statement}
Based on the above observations, the key research problem addressed in this work is how to reduce communication overhead in federated split learning systems while maintaining acceptable rainfall prediction performance. To address this gap, this work proposes a communication-efficient Federated Split Learning system for rainfall prediction using historical weather data collected from multiple locations around Newcastle upon Tyne through the Open-Meteo Historical Weather API. The proposed system introduces two complementary optimization strategies together with an adaptive control mechanism. First, a spatial optimization mechanism compresses smashed activations using quantization and Top-k sparsification techniques to reduce transmission size. Second, a temporal optimization mechanism introduces a tunable synchronization interval $\rho$ to regulate the frequency of global model aggregation. In addition, an adaptive scheduler dynamically adjusts these parameters based on runtime network conditions such as latency and bandwidth reported by clients. By jointly controlling communication size and frequency while adapting to network heterogeneity, the framework aims to reduce communication overhead while maintaining stable model convergence.
\subsection{Contributions}
The main contributions of this work are summarised as follows:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item A communication-efficient federated split learning framework for rainfall prediction using distributed IoT weather data.
    \item A dual optimisation strategy that reduces communication overhead through activation compression (quantisation and Top-k sparsification) and tunable synchronisation intervals.
    \item An adaptive scheduling mechanism that dynamically adjusts communication parameters based on runtime network conditions such as latency and bandwidth.
    \item A systematic experimental evaluation that analyses the trade-off between communication efficiency and prediction performance.
\end{enumerate}

The remainder of this paper is organised as follows. Section II reviews related work. Section III presents the proposed system design. Section IV describes the experimental setup. Section V discusses the evaluation results. Finally, Section VI concludes the paper.



\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:RelatedWork}
This section reviews existing studies related to communication-efficient distributed learning systems for IoT environments. Existing research can be broadly categorised into three directions: (1) communication-efficient Federated Split Learning, (2) adaptive scheduling and system-level optimisation, and (3) machine learning methods for rainfall prediction in resource-constrained environments.

\subsection{Communication-efficient Federated Split Learning}
Communication-efficient learning has become an important research topic in distributed machine learning, particularly in IoT and edge environments where bandwidth is limited. In traditional Federated Learning (FL), communication cost is mainly caused by frequent gradient or parameter exchange between clients and the server. To reduce transmission overhead, many studies focus on compression-based methods. For example, compressive sensingâ€“based approaches dynamically adjust compression ratios to reduce bidirectional communication cost \cite{liu2023fedcs}, while gradient quantisation and sparsification techniques are designed to address bandwidth constraints in wireless edge learning scenarios \cite{jin2023federated}. These methods show clear improvements in communication efficiency for conventional FL systems.

However, the communication pattern in Federated Split Learning (FSL) differs significantly from standard FL. Instead of exchanging full model gradients, clients send intermediate activations (also called smashed activations) to the server during the forward pass, and gradients are returned during the backward pass. As neural networks become deeper, the size of these activations can increase substantially, leading to high communication overhead. Therefore, compression techniques designed for traditional FL do not always directly address the communication characteristics of FSL.

Recent research attempts to improve efficiency in Split Learning and Federated Split Learning through architectural and synchronisation optimisation. Dynamic split point selection allows the training workload to be adjusted between client and server to reduce communication cost under different conditions \cite{mu2023communication}. Asynchronous training mechanisms relax strict synchronisation requirements to reduce communication bottlenecks \cite{Chen_2021}. Other works redesign the learning architecture to reduce server-side state dependency and improve scalability \cite{liang2025communicationandcomputationefficientsplitfederated}, \cite{liang2025communicationandcomputationefficientsplitfederated}, while adaptive aggregation strategies aim to balance communication cost and model convergence in distributed environments \cite{mahmoudi2024fedcau}. Although these methods improve efficiency, they often assume relatively stable network conditions or introduce additional system complexity.

\subsection{Adaptive Scheduling and System-Level Optimisation}
In addition to compression and architectural changes, another research direction focuses on adaptive scheduling and system-level optimisation for distributed learning. These approaches aim to improve performance by considering device capability, network latency, and resource availability during training \cite{liang2025communicationandcomputationefficientsplitfederated,mahmoudi2024fedcau}. 

Computation-oriented optimisation methods attempt to reduce the workload on resource-limited client devices. Techniques such as model pruning, adaptive layer reduction, and lightweight network design are widely used in edge learning scenarios. Adaptive pruning strategies dynamically adjust model complexity according to device capability to reduce computation cost \cite{10050151}. Lightweight prediction models are also designed to balance accuracy and efficiency in resource-constrained environments, particularly for time-series prediction tasks such as rainfall forecasting \cite{10.3389/frwa.2024.1378598}. 

However, these approaches mainly optimise computational cost rather than communication overhead. Even with smaller models, clients still participate in frequent synchronisation rounds, which can lead to high cumulative communication cost in distributed systems \cite{10078005}, \cite{2024ITWC...23.7362C}. In real IoT environments, network latency, bandwidth variation, and device heterogeneity may further increase training time and reduce system stability \cite{2023arXiv230205599M}, \cite{10522472}. 

Therefore, latency-aware training, resource-aware scheduling, and adaptive communication control are increasingly considered necessary for practical distributed learning systems \cite{2021arXiv210709786C}, \cite{liang2025communicationandcomputationefficientsplitfederated}. 
\subsection{Machine Learning for Rainfall Prediction in IoT Environments}
Machine learning has been widely applied to rainfall prediction and environmental sensing, especially with the increasing availability of IoT sensor data \cite{CHAN2023100375}. Lightweight prediction models are often used to improve efficiency while maintaining acceptable accuracy in resource-constrained environments \cite{10.3389/frwa.2024.1378598}. These methods demonstrate that machine learning can provide useful prediction capability for real-time monitoring systems. 

However, rainfall prediction remains a challenging task due to data imbalance, temporal variability, and the difficulty of predicting extreme events. Many studies focus primarily on improving modelling accuracy, without fully considering deployment constraints such as communication cost, device heterogeneity, and distributed training requirements. In real IoT sensing networks, prediction systems must operate under limited bandwidth, unstable connections, and heterogeneous devices, which makes communication efficiency as important as model accuracy \cite{10078005}, \cite{2024ITWC...23.7362C}. 

Across the three research directions above, a common limitation can be observed. Most existing studies optimise only one aspect of the system, such as compression, model structure, or distributed architecture \cite{10050151,2023arXiv230205599M,10522472}, without jointly considering data representation precision, activation size, and synchronisation frequency \cite{10.3389/frwa.2024.1378598,2021arXiv210709786C}. As a result, the interaction between communication cost, model convergence, and system scalability remains insufficiently explored, particularly for rainfall prediction systems deployed in real IoT environments. 

To address these limitations, this work proposes a communication-efficient Federated Split Learning system specifically tailored for real-world IoT sensor networks. Unlike existing studies that often treat compression and synchronization as isolated problems, our approach uniquely integrates activation compression with a tunable synchronization frequency to balance the trade-off between communication overhead and model convergence. Drawing upon foundational concepts in split learning optimization \cite{2023arXiv230205599M,liang2025communicationandcomputationefficientsplitfederated,Chen_2021}, we transition from theoretical simulation to a functional distributed system. By leveraging meteorological data retrieved from Open-Meteo for sensors located in Newcastle upon Tyne, the proposed system demonstrates how joint optimization of spatial feature compression and temporal communication control can effectively overcome hardware heterogeneity and unstable network conditions, thereby enhancing the overall practicality and scalability of decentralized environmental monitoring.
 
\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:Methodology}
\subsection{Split Learning Architecture}
Our system follows a federated split learning (FSL) architecture for rainfall prediction. Each client performs local sequence encoding, while the server completes prediction from transmitted smashed activations. This design reduces edge-side computation and preserves end-to-end trainability through activation and gradient exchange. For temporal modeling, the client-side \textit{ClientLSTM} (two-layer LSTM encoder) maps each weather window to a fixed-length representation, and the server-side \textit{ServerHead} performs joint rain-occurrence classification and rainfall-amount regression.

\subsubsection{Client-side Encoder}
Each client encodes a 24-step weather window with 5 features into a fixed-length smashed activation and sends only this intermediate representation to the server, instead of raw sequences. This preserves data locality and keeps the transmitted payload stable, which directly supports our communication-oriented compression and adaptive scheduling strategy.

\subsubsection{Server-side Prediction Head}
Beyond the standard split-learning pipeline, we introduce a shared multi-task server head that jointly models rain occurrence (classification) and rainfall amount (regression) from each smashed activation. This design is expected to improve task supervision under rainfall imbalance by activating the regression loss only for rainy samples, while keeping the transmitted cut-layer representation unchanged. Therefore, prediction quality can be improved without increasing per-step communication payload, and the server can still return cut-layer gradients for end-to-end split optimization under centralized supervision.

\subsubsection{Activation and Gradient Transfer}
For each forward step, the client sends compressed activation bytes and metadata (e.g., latency and payload statistics), and the server returns gradient bytes plus control signals for the next step. After local updates, model synchronization is executed every $\rho$ local epochs. Therefore, communication overhead in our FSL system consists of two coupled components: per-step activation/gradient round trips and periodic parameter synchronization, which motivates the adaptive scheduling and compression strategies described in the following subsections.


\subsection{Communication Bottleneck Analysis}
In our federated split learning system, communication cost mainly comes from two parts.
First, in each training step, the client sends smashed activations to the server and receives gradients back. This happens very frequently, so latency and payload size quickly accumulate.
Second, clients still need periodic model synchronisation with the server every $\rho$ local epochs, which adds extra upload and download traffic.

Therefore, communication efficiency depends on both data size and communication frequency. In unstable IoT networks, reducing only model complexity is not enough. We need to jointly control activation payloads and synchronisation intervals, which motivates our compression and adaptive scheduling design.

\subsection{Adaptive Scheduler}
In alignment with the adaptive communication control strategies, we design a rule-based adaptive scheduler that adjusts both compression mode and synchronisation interval ($\rho$) during training. Clients report latency-aware statistics to the server, and the scheduler applies predefined mapping rules to select the next communication strategy. Due to time and resource constraints, latency is generated by a profiler-based simulator in our experiments, and an exponential moving average (EMA) is used to smooth short-term fluctuations and avoid frequent policy switching. The scheduler changes only communication policies while keeping the model architecture unchanged. Specifically, lower latency keeps lightweight settings (e.g., float32 with smaller $\rho$), while higher latency triggers more aggressive settings (float16/int8/top-k with larger $\rho$). For example, when latency falls in the 4--10 ms range, the scheduler assigns float16 compression and $\rho=2$ (synchronisation every two local epochs).


\subsection{Compression Strategies}
We apply activation/gradient compression to both smashed activations and cut-layer gradients using four communication modes: float32, float16, int8, and top-k (implemented as top-k mode). Compression is evaluated in both static and adaptive settings: in static settings, one mode is fixed for the whole run, while in adaptive settings, the scheduler dynamically switches modes according to latency conditions. This design reduces payload size while allowing the system to balance communication efficiency and training stability under varying network conditions.

\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Data Acquisition and Preprocessing}
\label{subsec:dataset}
To validate the effectiveness of our adaptive framework, we conducted a case study using real-world meteorological observations retrieved from the Open-Meteo Historical Weather API. We simulated a distributed IoT sensing network by aggregating data from 12 distinct geographic coordinates across Newcastle upon Tyne, UK. Each virtual station provides a continuous time-series consisting of five core variables: Temperature, Humidity, Surface Pressure, Wind Speed, and Precipitation.

To simulate a realistic federated learning environment, we implement Non-IID data partitioning by assigning distinct sets of geographic sensors to each client. Specifically, the 12 weather stations are distributed among 3 clients, ensuring that each local model is trained on data with unique spatial characteristics and local climatic patterns.

For temporal modeling, we applied a 24-hour sliding-window strategy to capture the preceding meteorological context.
 The dataset is split chronologically, dedicating the final 14 days for testing and the preceding 14 days for validation to ensure unbiased performance evaluation. As shown in our statistical analysis, the dataset exhibits significant sparsity, with rainy events ($>0.1$mm) accounting for approximately 20.8\% of total observations. To address this class imbalance, our evaluation focuses on Recall and F1-score as primary metrics for event detection, alongside Mean Squared Error (MSE) and Mean Absolute Error (MAE) for assessing intensity regression accuracy.

\subsection{Multi-task Training and Configuration}
\label{subsec:training}
The multi-task learning framework described in Section \ref{sec:Methodology} is optimized using a consistent hyperparameter suite to ensure that performance variations across scenarios are attributable solely to communication strategies. We employ the Adam optimizer with a learning rate of $0.0005$ and a batch size of 32. The training process is executed for 30 global rounds, with each client performing 10 local iterations per round before a synchronization decision is triggered.

To normalize the high variance in rainfall intensity, the regression target is processed using a $\log(1+x)$ transformation. The joint objective function is weighted to prioritize rain event detection, assigning a coefficient of $2.0$ to the Focal Loss ($\gamma=2.0$, $\alpha=0.25$) and $1.0$ to the regression loss. During inference, we apply a classification-guided gating rule with a probability threshold of $0.34$; rainfall intensity is only predicted when the classifier identifies a high-probability event, thereby reducing false alarms in dry environments.

\subsection{System Configuration and Adaptive Control Settings}
\label{subsec:deployment}
The platform is implemented as a containerised environment using \textbf{Docker} and \textbf{gRPC} to simulate the orchestration and communication overhead of a distributed edge network. The experiments were executed on a dedicated hardware suite featuring an NVIDIA RTX 4090 Laptop GPU and 64GB of RAM to maintain sufficient compute throughput for parallel execution of all nodes.

In alignment with the adaptive communication control strategies discussed in Section \ref{sec:RelatedWork}, our proposed scheduler is initialized with empirical control settings derived from our configuration system. It is important to note that all parameters defined below serve as the initial state; the system dynamically escalates system-level optimisations during runtime based on EMA-smoothed latency:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Network Smoothing}: Latency estimation utilises an Exponential Moving Average (EMA) with a smoothing factor $\alpha_{ema} = 0.2$ to mitigate stochastic RTT noise.
    \item \textbf{Hierarchical Compression Thresholds}: Communication-oriented optimisation is triggered at three levels: \textbf{Float16} at 4.0ms, \textbf{INT8} at 10.0ms, and \textbf{Top-K} (density 0.5) at 15.0ms (calculated as 1.5$\times$ the INT8 threshold).
    \item \textbf{Dynamic Synchronisation Interval}: The synchronisation interval $\rho$ follows a step-wise growth logic. As network severity increases, $\rho$ scales from its base value up to a maximum safety limit of 20, reducing synchronisation frequency to preserve local compute autonomy and reduce cumulative communication costs.
\end{itemize}

\section{Results and Analysis}
\label{sec:Res}
This section evaluates the proposed communication-efficient FSL framework using the experiment matrix described in Section \ref{subsec:matrix}. We report results 10 random seeds and summarize each metric as mean $\pm$ standard deviation to reflect both performance and stability. Since rainfall events are relatively sparse, Macro-F1 is treated as the primary classification metric, while Micro-F1 and Accuracy are reported as complementary indicators. In addition, we analyze communication and system behavior using average payload size, reported latency, training duration, and server aggregation rounds.

The analysis is organized by research questions: (RQ1) overall performance versus baseline, (RQ2) the effect of compression strategies, (RQ3) the impact of synchronization interval $\rho$, (RQ4) adaptive behavior under medium, burst, and extreme latency profiles, and (RQ5) the final accuracy--efficiency trade-off. Unless otherwise stated, comparisons are made within matched latency settings to isolate the effect of each design component.

Under burst latency, M12 remains close to M11 in Macro-F1 while lowering payload, showing that adaptation is more beneficial when network variability is higher. Under extreme latency, M14 shortens runtime substantially compared with M13, but with increased payload and moderate F1 degradation, reflecting a different operating point in the efficiency--accuracy trade-off.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:Discussion}
\subsection{Imbalance task}
Rainfall prediction is inherently affected by class imbalance, as non-rainy periods dominate while rainfall events are relatively rare. This makes event detection particularly challenging, especially for recall-oriented tasks where missing rare events is costly. Although techniques such as focal loss, class weighting, threshold tuning, and rain-aware sampling are applied, these approaches remain manually designed and require careful tuning. Consequently, the current baseline should not be considered fully optimized. Instead, it serves primarily as a reference point for evaluating communication strategies. With more systematic approaches, such as principled resampling or advanced temporal models, both predictive accuracy and robustness could be significantly improved.
\subsection{Scheduler}
The proposed scheduler is rule-based and relies on profiler-generated latency signals. While this design is simple, interpretable, and practical, it is inherently limited in flexibility. The mapping between latency conditions and system responses is manually specified, which restricts its ability to adapt to unseen or highly dynamic environments. In reality, the interaction between latency, communication cost, and model convergence is often complex and non-linear. Therefore, more advanced approaches, such as learning-based schedulers using reinforcement learning or adaptive control, could provide more efficient and data-driven decision-making.
\subsection{Small-scale deployment}
The experimental setup is relatively small-scale compared to real-world distributed systems. Although multiple random seeds are used, the system includes only three clients and is entirely implemented in a Docker-based simulation environment. While this setup enables controlled experimentation, it does not fully capture real-world heterogeneity. In particular, the lack of deployment on physical edge devices (e.g., Raspberry Pi) means that factors such as limited computation, thermal constraints, and unstable wireless connections are not considered.

In addition, all experiments are conducted on a standard laptop environment, where system resources are shared with other background processes. This may introduce additional variability in runtime measurements, such as latency, due to resource contention between the main training process and other system tasks.
Therefore, the results should be interpreted as proof-of-concept rather than full real-world validation.
\subsection{No client dropout}
The experiments assume stable participation without client dropout, packet loss, or communication interruptions. However, such issues are common in real IoT systems due to unstable networks and device constraints. Since federated split learning depends on both activation exchange and synchronization, it can be sensitive to unreliable communication. As a result, the current evaluation may overestimate system robustness. Future work should consider more realistic scenarios, including partial participation, delayed updates, and communication failures.
\subsection{Dataset location specificity}
Finally, the dataset used in this study is collected from the Newcastle region, meaning that the learned patterns are inherently location-specific. Climate conditions, seasonal patterns, and weather dynamics can vary significantly across different regions. Therefore, the experimental findings may not directly generalize to other cities. Extending the evaluation to multiple geographic locations would be necessary to validate the robustness and generality of the proposed framework.

\section{Conclusion}
This paper introduces a communication-efficient Federated Split Learning (FSL) framework tailored for rainfall prediction in distributed IoT environments. By integrating spatial compression techniques (quantization and Top-k sparsification) with temporal synchronization control, the proposed system addresses the dual challenges of activation size and communication frequency inherent in FSL deployments.

The experimental evaluation across 14 scenarios (10 random seeds each) shows a consistent accuracy--efficiency trade-off rather than a single dominant policy. First, compression strategies such as INT8 quantization reduce payload by up to 73.4\% (256.0 bytes in M05 to 68.0 bytes in M06), with an average Macro-F1 reduction of about 0.038. Second, increasing the synchronization interval (from $\rho=1$ in M05 to $\rho=3$ in M08) cuts server rounds from 30 to 10 and shortens training time by 54.6\% (318.6s to 143.1s), with a moderate Macro-F1 decrease of 0.016. Third, adaptive scheduling changes policy according to runtime latency and is especially useful for reducing payload under harsher conditions (e.g., M12 shows a 20.7\% payload reduction with near-neutral Macro-F1 change), while runtime benefits are scenario-dependent (e.g., M14 is 63.4\% faster than M13 with a 0.022 Macro-F1 drop). Overall, pairwise comparisons indicate that compression mainly improves payload efficiency, larger $\rho$ mainly improves runtime, and adaptation mainly improves robustness to latency variation.

Overall, the results validate the framework's potential for practical IoT applications, where communication constraints are paramount. The framework maintains acceptable Macro-F1 scores (ranging from 0.429 to 0.478 across scenarios) while significantly reducing communication overhead. Future research directions include exploring reinforcement learning-based schedulers for more adaptive policy selection, larger-scale deployments with physical edge devices to capture real-world heterogeneity, and robustness enhancements against client dropout and packet loss to further advance communication-efficient distributed learning in real-world settings.
