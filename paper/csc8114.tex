% ---- Abstract ----
\begin{abstract}
  Federal Segmented Learning (FSL) has a bright future in Internet of Things rainfall forecasting because it can maintain the locality of data and reduce edge-end computing, but communication overhead is still a key deployment bottleneck. Existing studies usually optimise the activation compression and synchronisation frequencies respectively, but the research on their joint effects is not sufficient. We have proposed a communication-efficient FSL framework, which combines activation compression (float16/int8/top-k), Adjustable synchronous interval control ($\rho$), and an adaptive scheduler with delay perception function, which can adjust these two components at the same time. We used real meteorological data in non-IID 3 client environments to evaluate 14 situations, each using 10 random seeds. The results show that there is a consistent trade-off between accuracy and efficiency, rather than a single dominant strategy: stronger compression usually reduces the amount of data, but may reduce the macro F1 value; while larger $\rho$ will significantly reduce synchronisation overhead and training time, but will bring a certain degree of Loss of performance. Under more stringent latency conditions, adaptive control improves operating efficiency, but the impact of its running time and accuracy still depends on the specific scenario. These findings show that the actual FSL (Migration Learning) deployment should choose a communication strategy based on bandwidth, latency stability and acceptable accuracy decline, rather than optimising a single indicator.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

\subsection{Background}

With the rapid deployment of the Internet of Things (IoT) sensing network in the infrastructure of smart cities, a large amount of environmental data such as rainfall, temperature and humidity can be continuously collected through sensors distributed everywhere. \cite{GUBBI20131645}\cite{6740844}. In addition, historical meteorological records obtained through public data services (such as Open-Meteo historical weather API) further enrich the available meteorological data. These data sources provide important resources for data-based urban rainfall forecasting and environmental monitoring. \cite{CHAN2023100375}\cite{10.3389/frwa.2024.1378598}. However, the distributed characteristics of Internet of Things data make the traditional centralised machine learning method less suitable for practical applications, because the original sensor data must be frequently transmitted to the cloud server for model training. \cite{pmlr-v54-mcmahan17a}. This process may cause privacy issues and generate a lot of communication costs, especially in the case of limited bandwidth and unstable networks (applicable to Internet of Things networks). \cite{2019arXiv191204977K}.


To address these limitations, distributed learning paradigms such as federated learning (FL) and federated segmented learning (FSL) have been proposed. \cite{pmlr-v54-mcmahan17a,mu2023communication}. FL allows multiple clients to work together to train a shared model without directly sharing the raw data. \cite{pmlr-v54-mcmahan17a}, FSL, on the other hand, further reduces the computing burden of edge devices by dividing the neural network between the client and the central server. \cite{mu2023communication}. In this system, the client only calculates the early layer of the model and transmits the intermediate activation value to the server, while the server completes the remaining training process.\cite{mu2023communication,vepakomma2018splitlearninghealthdistributed}. Although this hybrid architecture performs well in protecting privacy and improving computing efficiency, it also brings new communication problems in the distributed Internet of Things environment. \cite{vepakomma2018splitlearninghealthdistributed}.


\subsection{Motivation}

However, in actual service-oriented machine learning applications, communication overhead is still one of the major challenges faced in solving problems related to real Internet of Things scenarios. \cite{mu2023communication,vepakomma2018splitlearninghealthdistributed}. In the traditional FL system, communication is mainly realised through the update of model parameters exchanged between the client and the server. \cite{pmlr-v54-mcmahan17a}. In contrast, FSL needs to transmit intermediate activation values and gradients in each training step, which will significantly increase the communication frequency and data volume. \cite{vepakomma2018splitlearninghealthdistributed}.


In the actual deployment of the Internet of Things, the network situation is often unstable, and problems such as delayed changes, packet loss or sensor failure will occur. These factors further increase the difficulty of designing a distributed learning system for efficient communication. \cite{2019arXiv191204977K}. Although this work does not clearly simulate packet loss or sensor failure, these practical constraints further highlight the importance of reducing communication overhead in distributed Internet of Things learning systems. \cite{2019arXiv191204977K}.


As neural networks become deeper and more complex, the size of transmitted activations can grow substantially \cite{vepakomma2018splitlearninghealthdistributed}. In addition, many existing systems treat synchronisation intervals as fixed parameters, resulting in frequent communication events that further increase network traffic.


These observations suggest that communication efficiency in FSL is influenced by two key factors: the size of transmitted activations and the frequency of synchronisation between clients and the server\cite{mu2023communication,vepakomma2018splitlearninghealthdistributed}. However, these two dimensions are often studied independently in existing literature, and few studies systematically investigate their combined impact on communication cost, convergence behaviour, and model performance in real-world IoT environments \cite{vepakomma2018splitlearninghealthdistributed}.


Furthermore, rainfall prediction in real-world IoT environments presents additional challenges, including data imbalance caused by the rarity of heavy rainfall events and unstable network conditions among distributed sensors \cite{CHAN2023100375}\cite{10.3389/frwa.2024.1378598}. These factors further motivate the need for communication-efficient and robust distributed learning systems.
Although the proposed system is generally applicable to a wide range of IoT-based prediction tasks, such as air quality monitoring, traffic flow prediction, and environmental forecasting \cite{6740844}, this study focuses on rainfall prediction as a representative application scenario. Rainfall prediction plays an important role in urban management, flood prevention, and early warning systems, particularly in cities where extreme weather events may cause significant disruptions \cite{CHAN2023100375}.


In addition, rainfall datasets collected from distributed weather sensors provide a suitable testbed for evaluating communication-efficient distributed learning methods in practical IoT environments \cite{6740844}\cite{10.3389/frwa.2024.1378598}.


\subsection{Problem Statement}
Based on the above observations, the key research problem addressed in this work is how to reduce communication overhead in federated split learning systems while maintaining acceptable rainfall prediction performance. To address this gap, this work proposes a communication-efficient Federated Split Learning system for rainfall prediction using historical weather data collected from multiple locations around Newcastle upon Tyne through the Open-Meteo Historical Weather API. The proposed system introduces two complementary optimisation strategies together with an adaptive control mechanism. First, a spatial optimisation mechanism compresses smashed activations using quantization and Top-k sparsification techniques to reduce transmission size. Second, a temporal optimisation mechanism introduces a tunable synchronisation interval $\rho$ to regulate the frequency of global model aggregation. In addition, an adaptive scheduler dynamically adjusts these parameters based on runtime network conditions such as latency and bandwidth reported by clients. By jointly controlling communication size and frequency while adapting to network heterogeneity, the framework aims to reduce communication overhead while maintaining stable model convergence.
\subsection{Contributions}
The main contributions of this work are summarised as follows:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item A communication-efficient federated split learning framework for rainfall prediction using distributed IoT weather data.
    \item A dual optimisation strategy that reduces communication overhead through activation compression (quantisation and Top-k sparsification) and tunable synchronisation intervals.
    \item An adaptive scheduling mechanism that dynamically adjusts communication parameters based on runtime network conditions such as latency and bandwidth.
    \item A systematic experimental evaluation that analyses the trade-off between communication efficiency and prediction performance.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Paper Organisation}
The remainder of this paper is organised as follows. Section II reviews related work. Section III presents the proposed system design. Section IV describes the experimental setup. Section V discusses the evaluation results. Finally, Section VI concludes the paper.



\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:RelatedWork}
This section reviews existing studies related to communication-efficient distributed learning systems for IoT environments. Existing research can be broadly categorised into three directions: (1) communication-efficient Federated Split Learning, (2) adaptive scheduling and system-level optimisation, and (3) machine learning methods for rainfall prediction in resource-constrained environments.

\subsection{Communication-efficient Federated Split Learning}
Communication-efficient learning has become an important research topic in distributed machine learning, particularly in IoT and edge environments where bandwidth is limited. In traditional Federated Learning (FL), communication cost is mainly caused by frequent gradient or parameter exchange between clients and the server. To reduce transmission overhead, many studies focus on compression-based methods. For example, the method based on compression perception can dynamically adjust the compression ratio to reduce the overhead of two-way communication. \cite{10078005}, Although gradient quantisation and sparseness techniques are designed to solve the problem of bandwidth limitation in wireless edge learning scenarios. But these methods have significantly improved communication efficiency in traditional federal learning systems.

However, the communication mode in federal segmented learning (FSL) is significantly different from standard federal learning (FL). In the standard FL, the client will exchange the complete model gradient; in FSL, the client sends an intermediate activation value (also known as "broken activation value") to the server during forward propagation, and returns to the gradient during reverse propagation. As the neural network becomes deeper and deeper, the size of these activation values will increase significantly, resulting in huge communication overhead. Therefore, the compression technology designed for traditional FL does not always directly solve the communication characteristics of FSL.

Recent research is dedicated to improving the efficiency of "split learning" (SL) and "limited learning" (FSL). For example, the selection of dynamic split points helps to balance the workload. \cite{mu2023communication}, the asynchronous method can alleviate the network bottleneck. \cite{Chen_2021}. Other works have redesigned the architecture to improve scalability. \cite{2026ITWC...25.1981L}. Based on these concepts, we propose an adaptive communication strategy, which can dynamically adjust the aggregation frequency and compression level to balance data transmission and model accuracy. The previous method usually assumes that the network configuration is fixed, while our method uses a lightweight dispatcher to adapt to changing delay and bandwidth conditions, so as to ensure the high efficiency of the system while avoiding excessive computational complexity.

\subsection{Adaptive Scheduling and System-Level Optimisation}
In addition to compression and architectural changes, another research direction focuses on adaptive scheduling and system-level optimisation for distributed learning. These approaches aim to improve performance by considering device capability, network latency, and resource availability during training \cite{2026ITWC...25.1981L,10478872}. 

Computation-oriented optimisation methods attempt to reduce the workload on resource-limited client devices. Techniques such as model pruning, adaptive layer reduction, and lightweight network design are widely used in edge learning scenarios. Adaptive pruning strategies dynamically adjust model complexity according to device capability to reduce computation cost \cite{10050151}. Lightweight prediction models are also designed to balance accuracy and efficiency in resource-constrained environments, particularly for time-series prediction tasks such as rainfall forecasting. 

However, these approaches mainly optimise computational cost rather than communication overhead. Even with smaller models, clients still participate in frequent synchronisation rounds, which can lead to high cumulative communication cost in distributed systems \cite{10078005}, \cite{2024ITWC...23.7362C}. In real IoT environments, network latency, bandwidth variation, and device heterogeneity may further increase training time and reduce system stability \cite{mu2023communication}, \cite{10522472}. 

Therefore, latency-aware training, resource-aware scheduling, and adaptive communication control are increasingly considered necessary for practical distributed learning systems \cite{Chen_2021}, \cite{2026ITWC...25.1981L}. 
\subsection{Machine Learning for Rainfall Prediction in IoT Environments}
Machine learning has been widely applied to rainfall prediction and environmental sensing, especially with the increasing availability of IoT sensor data \cite{saeed2021rainfall}. Lightweight forecasting models are often used to maintain acceptable accuracy while improving efficiency in resource-limited environments. These methods show that machine learning can provide useful predictive capabilities for real-time monitoring systems. 

However, rainfall forecasting is still a very challenging task due to data imbalance, time variability and the difficulty of predicting extreme events. Many studies mainly focus on improving the accuracy of the model, but do not fully consider deployment constraints such as communication costs, device heterogeneity and distributed training requirements. In the actual Internet of Things sensing network, the prediction system must operate in the environment of limited bandwidth, unstable connection and heterogeneous devices, which makes communication efficiency as important as model accuracy. \cite{10078005}, \cite{2024ITWC...23.7362C}. 

Among the above three research directions, a common limitation can be found. Most existing studies only optimise one aspect of the system, such as compression, model structure or distributed architecture. \cite{10050151,mu2023communication,10522472}, because the accuracy of data representation, the size of activation and the frequency of synchronisation are not considered at the same time. Therefore, the interrelationship between communication cost, model convergence and system scalability has not been fully studied, especially in rainfall forecasting systems deployed in the actual Internet of Things environment. 

In order to solve these limitations, this study proposes a federal split learning system with high communication efficiency, which is specially designed for the real-world Internet of Things sensor networks. Unlike existing studies that usually regard compression and synchronisation as independent problems, our method uniquely combines activated compression with adjustable synchronisation frequencies to balance the trade-off between communication overhead and model convergence. Drawing on the basic concept of segmented learning optimisation \cite{mu2023communication,2026ITWC...25.1981L,Chen_2021}, We have transitioned from theoretical simulation to a functional distributed system. By using meteorological data obtained from Open-Meteo to provide information for sensors in Newcastle Tyne, the proposed system shows how to jointly optimise spatial feature compression and time communication control, so as to effectively overcome hardware heterogeneity and unstable network conditions, and then raise The overall practicality and scalability of highly decentralised environmental monitoring.
 
\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:Methodology}
\subsection{Split Learning Architecture}
Our system follows a federated split learning (FSL) architecture for rainfall prediction. Each client performs local sequence encoding, while the server completes prediction from transmitted smashed activations. This design reduces edge-side computation and preserves end-to-end trainability through activation and gradient exchange. For temporal modelling, the client-side \textit{ClientLSTM} (two-layer LSTM encoder) maps each weather window to a fixed-length representation, and the server-side \textit{ServerHead} performs joint rain-occurrence classification and rainfall-amount regression.

\subsubsection{Client-side Encoder}
Each client encodes a 24-step weather window with 5 features into a fixed-length smashed activation and sends only this intermediate representation to the server, instead of raw sequences. This preserves data locality and keeps the transmitted payload stable, which directly supports our communication-oriented compression and adaptive scheduling strategy.

\subsubsection{Server-side Prediction Head}
Beyond the standard split-learning pipeline, we introduce a shared multi-task server head that jointly models rain occurrence (classification) and rainfall amount (regression) from each smashed activation. This design is expected to improve task supervision under rainfall imbalance by activating the regression loss only for rainy samples, while keeping the transmitted cut-layer representation unchanged. Therefore, prediction quality can be improved without increasing per-step communication payload, and the server can still return cut-layer gradients for end-to-end split optimisation under centralized supervision.

\subsubsection{Activation and Gradient Transfer}
For each forward step, the client sends compressed activation bytes and metadata (e.g., latency and payload statistics), and the server returns gradient bytes plus control signals for the next step. After local updates, model synchronisation is executed every $\rho$ local epochs. Therefore, communication overhead in our FSL system consists of two coupled components: per-step activation/gradient round trips and periodic parameter synchronisation, which motivates the adaptive scheduling and compression strategies described in the following subsections.


\subsection{Communication Bottleneck Analysis}
In our federated split learning system, communication cost mainly comes from two parts.
First, in each training step, the client sends smashed activations to the server and receives gradients back. This happens very frequently, so latency and payload size quickly accumulate.
Second, clients still need periodic model synchronisation with the server every $\rho$ local epochs, which adds extra upload and download traffic.

Therefore, communication efficiency depends on both data size and communication frequency. In unstable IoT networks, reducing only model complexity is not enough. We need to jointly control activation payloads and synchronisation intervals, which motivates our compression and adaptive scheduling design.

\subsection{Adaptive Scheduler}
In alignment with the adaptive communication control strategies, we design a rule-based adaptive scheduler that adjusts both compression mode and synchronisation interval ($\rho$) during training. Clients report latency-aware statistics to the server, and the scheduler applies predefined mapping rules to select the next communication strategy. Due to time and resource constraints, latency is generated by a profiler-based simulator in our experiments, and an exponential moving average (EMA) is used to smooth short-term fluctuations and avoid frequent policy switching. The scheduler changes only communication policies while keeping the model architecture unchanged. Specifically, lower latency keeps lightweight settings (e.g., float32 with smaller $\rho$), while higher latency triggers more aggressive settings (float16/int8/top-k with larger $\rho$). For example, when latency falls in the 4--10 ms range, the scheduler assigns float16 compression and $\rho=2$ (synchronisation every two local epochs).


\subsection{Compression Strategies}
We apply activation/gradient compression to both smashed activations and cut-layer gradients using four communication modes: float32, float16, int8, and top-k (implemented as top-k mode). Compression is evaluated in both static and adaptive settings: in static settings, one mode is fixed for the whole run, while in adaptive settings, the scheduler dynamically switches modes according to latency conditions. This design reduces payload size while allowing the system to balance communication efficiency and training stability under varying network conditions.

\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Data Acquisition and Preprocessing}
\label{subsec:dataset}
In order to verify the effectiveness of our adaptive system, we conducted a case study using real meteorological observation data obtained from the Open-Meteo historical weather API. The API is supported by high-resolution reanalysis models such as ERA5. \cite{hersbach2020era5}. We simulated a distributed IoT sensing network by aggregating data from 12 distinct geographic coordinates across Newcastle upon Tyne, UK.

Each virtual site will provide a continuous time series data, including five key indicators: temperature, humidity, surface pressure, wind speed and precipitation. Follow the feature selection specification in ERA5\cite{hersbach2020era5} and WeatherBench \cite{rasp2020weatherbench}, The selection of these characteristics aims to cover the dynamic causes and historical patterns of rainfall. Temperature and humidity determine the capacity of moisture, while surface pressure and wind speed drive atmospheric movement, and taking into account historical rainfall allows the model to capture self-correlation in time. \cite{saeed2021rainfall}. These five variables work together to form a comprehensive multi-dimensional feature description, enabling the FSL system to distinguish between short showers and continuous frontal rainfall.

In order to simulate a real federal learning environment, we realise the division of non-distributed data by assigning different sets of geosensors to each client. Specifically, 12 weather stations are assigned to 3 clients to ensure that each local model is trained based on data with unique spatial characteristics and local climate patterns.

For the construction of the time model, we adopted a 24-hour sliding window strategy to capture previous meteorological background information, aiming to predict the cumulative precipitation in the next 3-hour window. The data set is divided in chronological order to maintain the time integrity of the weather pattern: the last 14 days are left as test sets, and the 14 days immediately after that are left as verification sets. This chronological division ensures the fairness of performance evaluation and avoids data leakage.

As shown in our statistical analysis, the dataset exhibits significant sparsity, with measurable precipitation ($>0$\,mm) accounting for only $20.8\%$ of total observations. For model training and evaluation, we apply a $0.1$\,mm detection threshold to define a ``rainy event,'' aligning with the WMO standard for minimum measurable precipitation to filter out sensor noise \cite{wmo2018guide}. Due to this class imbalance, our evaluation prioritises Recall and F1-score for event detection, complemented by Mean Squared Error (MSE) and Mean Absolute Error (MAE) for assessing regression accuracy. 

\subsection{Multi-task Training and Configuration}
\label{subsec:training}
The multi-task learning framework described in Section \ref{sec:Methodology} is optimised using a consistent hyperparameter suite to ensure that performance variations across scenarios are attributable solely to communication strategies. We employ the Adam optimiser with a learning rate of $0.0005$ and a batch size of 32. The training process is executed for 30 global rounds, with each client performing 10 local iterations per round before a synchronisation decision is triggered.

To normalise the high variance in rainfall intensity, the regression target is processed using a $\log(1+x)$ transformation. The joint objective function is weighted to prioritise rain event detection, assigning a coefficient of $2.0$ to the Focal Loss ($\gamma=2.0$, $\alpha=0.25$) and $1.0$ to the regression loss. During inference, we apply a classification-guided gating rule with a probability threshold of $0.34$; rainfall intensity is only predicted when the classifier identifies a high-probability event, thereby reducing false alarms in dry environments.

\subsection{System Configuration and Adaptive Control Settings}
\label{subsec:deployment}
The platform is implemented as a containerised environment using Docker and gRPC to simulate the orchestration and communication overhead of a distributed edge network. The experiments were executed on a standard Laptop with NVIDIA RTX 4090 GPU and 64GB of RAM to maintain sufficient compute throughput for parallel execution of all nodes.

In alignment with the adaptive communication control strategies discussed in Section \ref{sec:RelatedWork}, our proposed scheduler is initialised with empirical control settings derived from our configuration system. It is important to note that all parameters defined below serve as the initial state; the system dynamically escalates system-level optimisations during runtime based on EMA-smoothed latency:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Network Smoothing}: Latency estimation utilises an Exponential Moving Average (EMA) with a smoothing factor $\alpha_{ema} = 0.2$ to mitigate stochastic RTT noise.
    \item \textbf{Hierarchical Compression Thresholds}: Communication-oriented optimisation is triggered at three levels: \textbf{Float16} at 4.0ms, \textbf{INT8} at 10.0ms, and \textbf{Top-K} (density 0.5) at 15.0ms (calculated as 1.5$\times$ the INT8 threshold).
    \item \textbf{Dynamic Synchronisation Interval}: The synchronisation interval $\rho$ follows a step-wise growth logic. As network severity increases, $\rho$ scales from its base value up to a maximum safety limit of 20, reducing synchronisation frequency to preserve local compute autonomy and reduce cumulative communication costs.
\end{itemize}

\section{Results and Analysis}
\label{sec:Res}
This section evaluates the proposed communication-efficient FSL framework using the experiment matrix described in Section \ref{subsec:matrix}. We report results over 10 random seeds and summarise each metric as mean $\pm$ standard deviation to reflect both performance and stability. Since rainfall events are relatively sparse, Macro-F1 is treated as the primary classification metric, while Micro-F1 and Accuracy are reported as complementary indicators. In addition, we analyse communication and system behaviour using average payload size, reported latency, training duration, and server aggregation rounds.

The analysis is organised by research questions: (RQ1) overall performance versus baseline, (RQ2) the effect of compression strategies, (RQ3) the impact of synchronisation interval $\rho$, (RQ4) adaptive behaviour under medium, burst, and extreme latency profiles, and (RQ5) the final accuracy--efficiency trade-off. Unless otherwise stated, comparisons are made within matched latency settings to isolate the effect of each design component.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:Discussion}
\subsection{Imbalance task}
Rainfall prediction is inherently affected by class imbalance, as non-rainy periods dominate while rainfall events are relatively rare. In this study, our objective is not to maximize absolute predictive accuracy, but to isolate the communication--performance trade-off under a fixed model backbone and training protocol. Therefore, in order to ensure a fair comparison of communication strategies rather than performance adjustments for specific tasks, the imbalance processing components (focus/category weighting, threshold setting and oversampling of rainy day perception) are intentionally kept unchanged in different scenarios. This makes event detection challenging, especially in recall-oriented scenarios, because missing rare events will bring high costs. Therefore, the current benchmark should be regarded as a control reference for communication analysis, and future work can further improve the predictive performance through more systematic model and data level optimisation.

\subsection{Scheduler}
The proposed scheduler is rule-based and relies on the generated delay signal. Although this design is simple, easy to understand and practical, there are inherent limitations in its flexibility. The mapping between the delay condition and the system response is manually specified, which limits its ability to adapt to unknown or highly dynamic environments. In fact, the interaction between latency, communication cost and model convergence is usually complex and nonlinear. Therefore, more advanced methods, such as the use of reinforcement learning or adaptively controlled learning-based schedulers, can provide more efficient and data-based decision-making.\subsection{Small-scale deployment}
Compared with the actual distributed system, the scale of the experimental setting is relatively small. Despite the use of multiple random seeds, the system contains only three clients and is completely implemented in a Docker-based simulation environment. Although this setting can carry out controllable experiments, it does not fully reflect the heterogeneity of the real world. In particular, because it is not deployed on physical edge devices (such as Raspberry Pi), factors such as limited computing power, thermal limitation and unstable wireless connection are not taken into account.

In addition, all experiments are carried out in a standard laptop environment, in which system resources will be shared with other background processes. This may introduce additional variability in the runtime measurement results (such as delay) due to resource competition between the main training process and other system tasks.
Therefore, these results should be regarded as proof of concept rather than complete real-world verification.
\subsection{No client dropout}
These experiments assume that the participation process is stable, and there is no client exit, packet loss or communication interruption. However, in the actual Internet of Things system, such problems are very common due to network instability and equipment limitations. Since federal split learning relies on the exchange and synchronisation of activated information, it may be very sensitive to unreliable communication. Therefore, the current assessment may overestimate the reliability of the system. Future research should consider scenarios that are closer to the actual situation, including partial participation, delayed updates and communication failures.
\subsection{Dataset location specificity}
Finally, the data set used in this study comes from the Newcastle area, which means that the model learned has obvious geographical characteristics. The climatic conditions, seasonal changes and weather dynamics may vary greatly from region to region. Therefore, the experimental results may not be directly popularised to other cities. It is necessary to extend the assessment to multiple geographical regions to verify the reliability and universality of the proposed framework.

\section{Conclusion}
This article introduces a federal segmented learning (FSL) framework with high communication efficiency, which is specially designed for rainfall forecasting in distributed Internet of Things environments. By combining spatial compression technology (quantification and Top-k sparseness) with time synchronisation control, the proposed system solves the dual challenges of activation size and communication frequency inherent in federal segmented learning deployment.

In the experimental evaluation of 14 scenarios (each scene has 10 random seeds), a consistent trade-off between accuracy and efficiency is shown, rather than a single dominant strategy. First of all, compression strategies such as INT8 quantisation can reduce the amount of data by up to 73.4% (from 256.0 bytes of M05 to 68.0 bytes of M06), and the average Macro-F1 is reduced by about 0.038. Secondly, increasing the synchronisation interval (from $\rho=1$ in M05 to $\rho=3$ in M08) can reduce the number of server rounds from 30 to 10 and shorten the training time by 54.6% (from 318.6 seconds to 143.1 Seconds), at the same time, Macro-F1 is moderately reduced by 0.016. Third, adaptive scheduling changes policy according to runtime latency and is especially useful for reducing payload under harsher conditions (e.g., M12 shows a 20.7\% payload reduction with near-neutral Macro-F1 change), while runtime benefits are scenario-dependent (e.g., M14 is 63.4\% faster than M13 with a 0.022 Macro-F1 drop). Overall, pairwise comparisons indicate that compression mainly improves payload efficiency, larger $\rho$ mainly improves runtime, and adaptation mainly improves robustness to latency variation.

Overall, the results validate the framework's potential for practical IoT applications, where communication constraints are paramount. The framework maintains acceptable Macro-F1 scores (ranging from 0.429 to 0.478 across scenarios) while significantly reducing communication overhead. Future research directions include exploring reinforcement learning-based schedulers for more adaptive policy selection, larger-scale deployments with physical edge devices to capture real-world heterogeneity, and robustness enhancements against client dropout and packet loss to further advance communication-efficient distributed learning in real-world settings.
