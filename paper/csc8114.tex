% ---- Abstract ----
\begin{abstract}
  Federated Split Learning (FSL) is a promising paradigm for IoT rainfall prediction because it preserves data locality and reduces edge side computation, but communication overhead remains a major deployment bottleneck. Existing studies often optimise activation compression and synchronisation frequency separately, leaving their joint effect underexplored. We propose an FSL framework that combines activation compression (float16/int8/top-k), tunable synchronisation interval control ($\rho$), and a latency aware adaptive scheduler that adjusts both dimensions jointly at runtime. Using real meteorological data from 12 distributed sensors partitioned among 3 clients in a non-IID setting where each client utilises five key features, we evaluate 14 scenarios with 10 random seeds each. Results show a consistent accuracy efficiency trade-off rather than a single dominant policy: stronger compression generally reduces payload but may reduce Macro F1, while larger $\rho$ substantially reduces synchronisation rounds and system runtime with moderate performance loss. Under harsher latency profiles, adaptive control improves operating efficiency, though runtime and accuracy effects remain scenario dependent. Overall, practical FSL deployment should choose communication and computation policies according to bandwidth budget, latency stability, and acceptable accuracy degradation, instead of optimising only one metric.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

\subsection{Background}

With the rapid deployment of Internet of Things (IoT) sensing networks in smart city infrastructure, large volumes of environmental observations such as rainfall, temperature, and humidity can be continuously collected by geographically distributed sensors \cite{GUBBI20131645,6740844}. In addition, historical meteorological records from public services such as the Open Meteo Historical Weather API further enrich available data resources. These data sources provide an important basis for data-driven urban rainfall prediction and environmental monitoring \cite{CHAN2023100375,10.3389/frwa.2024.1378598}. However, the distributed nature of IoT data makes traditional centralised machine learning less suitable in practice, since raw sensor data must be frequently transmitted to cloud servers for model training \cite{pmlr-v54-mcmahan17a}. This process introduces privacy concerns and substantial communication cost, especially under bandwidth constrained and unstable IoT networks \cite{2019arXiv191204977K}.


To address these limitations, distributed learning paradigms such as Federated Learning (FL) and Federated Split Learning (FSL) have been proposed \cite{pmlr-v54-mcmahan17a,mu2023communication}. FL enables multiple clients to collaboratively train a shared model without directly sharing raw data \cite{pmlr-v54-mcmahan17a}. FSL further reduces client side computation by splitting the neural network between clients and a central server \cite{mu2023communication}. In this architecture, clients compute only early layers and transmit intermediate activations to the server, which completes the remaining training process \cite{mu2023communication,vepakomma2018splitlearninghealthdistributed}. Although this hybrid design improves privacy preservation and edge side efficiency, it also introduces new communication bottlenecks in distributed IoT environments \cite{vepakomma2018splitlearninghealthdistributed}.


\subsection{Motivation}

Communication overhead remains one of the main barriers to practical FSL deployment in real IoT settings \cite{mu2023communication,vepakomma2018splitlearninghealthdistributed}. In conventional FL, communication is dominated by periodic model parameter exchange \cite{pmlr-v54-mcmahan17a}. In contrast, FSL requires activation and gradient exchange at each training step, which can significantly increase both communication frequency and payload volume \cite{vepakomma2018splitlearninghealthdistributed}.


In real IoT deployments, network conditions are often unstable, with variable latency, packet loss, and occasional sensor failures \cite{2019arXiv191204977K}. Although this work does not explicitly simulate packet loss or sensor failures, such constraints further highlight the need for communication efficient distributed learning.


As neural networks become deeper and more complex, the size of transmitted activations can grow substantially \cite{vepakomma2018splitlearninghealthdistributed}. In addition, many existing systems treat synchronisation intervals as fixed parameters, resulting in frequent communication events that further increase network traffic.


These observations suggest that communication efficiency in FSL is influenced by two key factors: the size of transmitted activations and the frequency of synchronisation between clients and the server \cite{mu2023communication,vepakomma2018splitlearninghealthdistributed}. However, these two dimensions are often studied independently in existing literature, and few studies systematically investigate their combined impact on communication cost, convergence behaviour, and model performance in real world IoT environments \cite{vepakomma2018splitlearninghealthdistributed}.


Furthermore, rainfall prediction in real world IoT environments presents additional challenges, including data imbalance caused by the rarity of heavy rainfall events and unstable network conditions among distributed sensors \cite{CHAN2023100375,10.3389/frwa.2024.1378598}. These factors further motivate the need for communication efficient and robust distributed learning systems.
Although the proposed system is generally applicable to a wide range of IoT based prediction tasks, such as air quality monitoring, traffic flow prediction, and environmental forecasting \cite{6740844}, this study focuses on rainfall prediction as a representative application scenario. Rainfall prediction plays an important role in urban management, flood prevention, and early warning systems, particularly in cities where extreme weather events may cause significant disruptions \cite{CHAN2023100375}.


In addition, rainfall datasets collected from distributed weather sensors provide a suitable testbed for evaluating communication efficient distributed learning methods in practical IoT environments \cite{6740844,10.3389/frwa.2024.1378598}.


\subsection{Problem Statement}
Based on the above observations, the key research problem addressed in this work is how to reduce communication overhead and system level computation burden in federated split learning while maintaining acceptable rainfall prediction performance. To address this gap, this work proposes a communication efficient Federated Split Learning system for rainfall prediction using historical weather data collected from multiple locations around Newcastle upon Tyne through the Open Meteo Historical Weather API. The proposed system introduces two complementary optimisation strategies together with an adaptive control mechanism. First, a spatial optimisation mechanism compresses smashed activations using quantisation and Top-k sparsification techniques to reduce transmission size. Second, a temporal optimisation mechanism introduces a tunable synchronisation interval $\rho$ to regulate the frequency of global model aggregation. In addition, an adaptive scheduler dynamically adjusts these parameters based on runtime network conditions such as latency and bandwidth reported by clients. By jointly controlling communication size and synchronisation frequency while adapting to network heterogeneity, the framework aims to improve communication efficiency and reduce end to end runtime overhead.
\subsection{Contributions}
The main contributions of this work are summarised as follows:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item A communication efficient federated split learning framework for rainfall prediction using distributed IoT weather data.
    \item A joint computation communication optimisation strategy that combines activation compression (quantisation and Top-k sparsification) with tunable synchronisation intervals to reduce payload and server aggregation workload.
    \item An adaptive scheduling mechanism that dynamically adjusts communication parameters based on runtime network conditions such as latency and bandwidth.
    \item A systematic experimental evaluation that analyses the trade-off among communication efficiency, system runtime (as a computation proxy), and prediction performance.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Paper Organisation}
The remainder of this paper is organised as follows. Section II reviews related work. Section III presents the proposed system design. Section IV describes the experimental setup. Section V discusses the evaluation results. Finally, Section VI concludes the paper.



\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:RelatedWork}
This section reviews existing studies related to communication efficient distributed learning systems for IoT environments. Existing research can be broadly categorised into three directions: (1) communication efficient Federated Split Learning, (2) adaptive scheduling and system level optimisation, and (3) machine learning methods for rainfall prediction in resource constrained environments.

\subsection{Communication efficient Federated Split Learning}
Communication efficiency is a key research topic in distributed machine learning, especially in IoT and edge environments with limited bandwidth. In conventional FL, communication cost is mainly caused by frequent gradient or parameter exchange between clients and the server. To reduce transmission overhead, many studies propose compression oriented methods, including adaptive compression ratio control, quantisation, and sparsification \cite{10078005}. These approaches improve communication efficiency in standard FL systems.

However, the communication pattern in FSL differs from standard FL. Instead of exchanging full model gradients, clients send intermediate activations (smashed activations) to the server during the forward pass, and gradients are returned during the backward pass. As networks become deeper, activation tensors can grow significantly, leading to substantial communication overhead. Therefore, techniques designed for standard FL do not always transfer directly to FSL.

Recent work has explored more efficient split learning systems. Examples include dynamic split point selection for workload balancing \cite{mu2023communication}, asynchronous designs to reduce straggler effects \cite{Chen_2021}, and architecture redesign for scalability \cite{2026ITWC...25.1981L}. Building on these directions, our work focuses on adaptive communication control by jointly adjusting compression and synchronisation behaviour under variable latency and bandwidth conditions.

\subsection{Adaptive Scheduling and System Level Optimisation}
In addition to compression and architectural changes, another research direction focuses on adaptive scheduling and system level optimisation for distributed learning. These approaches aim to improve performance by considering device capability, network latency, and resource availability during training \cite{2026ITWC...25.1981L,10478872}. 

Computation oriented optimisation methods attempt to reduce the workload on resource limited client devices. Techniques such as model pruning, adaptive layer reduction, and lightweight network design are widely used in edge learning scenarios. Adaptive pruning strategies dynamically adjust model complexity according to device capability to reduce computation cost \cite{10050151}. Lightweight prediction models are also designed to balance accuracy and efficiency in resource constrained environments, particularly for time series prediction tasks such as rainfall forecasting. 

However, these approaches mainly optimise computational cost rather than communication overhead. Even with smaller models, clients still participate in frequent synchronisation rounds, which can lead to high cumulative communication cost in distributed systems \cite{10078005,2024ITWC...23.7362C}. In real IoT environments, network latency, bandwidth variation, and device heterogeneity may further increase training time and reduce system stability \cite{mu2023communication,10522472}. 

Therefore, latency aware training, resource aware scheduling, and adaptive communication control are increasingly considered necessary for practical distributed learning systems \cite{Chen_2021,2026ITWC...25.1981L}. 
\subsection{Machine Learning for Rainfall Prediction in IoT Environments}
Machine learning has been widely applied to rainfall prediction and environmental sensing, especially with the increasing availability of IoT sensor data \cite{saeed2021rainfall}. Lightweight forecasting models are often used to maintain acceptable accuracy while improving efficiency in resource limited environments. These methods show that machine learning can provide useful predictive capabilities for real time monitoring systems. 

However, rainfall prediction remains challenging due to class imbalance, temporal variability, and the difficulty of forecasting extreme events. Many existing studies focus primarily on model accuracy, while giving limited attention to deployment constraints such as communication overhead, device heterogeneity, and distributed training dynamics. In practical IoT sensing networks, prediction systems operate under limited bandwidth and unstable links, making system efficiency as important as predictive quality \cite{10078005,2024ITWC...23.7362C}.

Across the three directions above, a common limitation is that many studies optimise only one system dimension at a time (e.g., compression, architecture, or scheduling) \cite{10050151,mu2023communication,10522472}. The joint interaction among representation precision, activation payload, synchronisation frequency, and runtime behaviour remains insufficiently explored for real IoT rainfall prediction.

To address this gap, we propose a communication efficient FSL system tailored to real world IoT weather sensing. Unlike approaches that treat compression and synchronisation independently, our method integrates activation compression with tunable synchronisation control and latency aware adaptation. Using Open Meteo data from sensors around Newcastle upon Tyne, we demonstrate how joint spatial temporal communication control can improve deployment practicality under heterogeneous and unstable network conditions.
 
\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:Methodology}
\subsection{Split Learning Architecture}
Our system follows a federated split learning (FSL) architecture for rainfall prediction. Each client performs local sequence encoding, while the server completes prediction from transmitted smashed activations. This design reduces edge side computation and preserves end to end trainability through activation and gradient exchange. For temporal modelling, the client side \textit{ClientLSTM} (two layer LSTM encoder) maps each weather window to a fixed length representation, and the server side \textit{ServerHead} performs joint rain occurrence classification and rainfall amount regression.

\subsubsection{Client side Encoder}
Each client encodes a 24 step weather window with 5 features into a fixed length smashed activation and sends only this intermediate representation to the server, instead of raw sequences. This preserves data locality and keeps the transmitted payload stable, which directly supports our communication oriented compression and adaptive scheduling strategy.

\subsubsection{Server side Prediction Head}
Beyond the standard split learning pipeline, we introduce a shared multitask server head that jointly models rain occurrence (classification) and rainfall amount (regression) from each smashed activation. This design is expected to improve task supervision under rainfall imbalance by activating the regression loss only for rainy samples, while keeping the transmitted cut layer representation unchanged. Therefore, prediction quality can be improved without increasing per step communication payload, and the server can still return cut layer gradients for end to end split optimisation under centralised supervision.

\subsubsection{Activation and Gradient Transfer}
For each forward step, the client sends compressed activation bytes and metadata (e.g., latency and payload statistics), and the server returns gradient bytes plus control signals for the next step. After local updates, model synchronisation is executed every $\rho$ local epochs. Therefore, communication overhead in our FSL system consists of two coupled components: per step activation/gradient round trips and periodic parameter synchronisation, which motivates the adaptive scheduling and compression strategies described in the following subsections.


\subsection{Communication Bottleneck Analysis}
In our federated split learning system, communication cost mainly comes from two parts.
First, in each training step, the client sends smashed activations to the server and receives gradients back. This happens very frequently, so latency and payload size quickly accumulate.
Second, clients still need periodic model synchronisation with the server every $\rho$ local epochs, which adds extra upload and download traffic.

Therefore, communication efficiency depends on both data size and communication frequency. In unstable IoT networks, reducing only model complexity is not enough. We need to jointly control activation payloads and synchronisation intervals, which motivates our compression and adaptive scheduling design.

From a computation perspective, this design also affects training workload at the system level: larger synchronisation intervals reduce server aggregation operations, and split learning keeps most heavy layers on the server side, reducing client side compute pressure. In this paper, we report runtime and server rounds as practical computation related proxies, while acknowledging that direct FLOPs and energy profiling are left for future work.

\subsection{Adaptive Scheduler}
In alignment with the adaptive communication control strategies, we design a rule-based adaptive scheduler that adjusts both compression mode and synchronisation interval ($\rho$) during training. Clients report latency aware statistics to the server, and the scheduler applies predefined mapping rules to select the next communication strategy. Due to time and resource constraints, latency is generated by a profiler-based simulator in our experiments, and an exponential moving average (EMA) is used to smooth short-term fluctuations and avoid frequent policy switching. The scheduler changes only communication policies while keeping the model architecture unchanged. Specifically, lower latency keeps lightweight settings (e.g., float32 with smaller $\rho$), while higher latency triggers more aggressive settings (float16/int8/top-k with larger $\rho$). For example, when latency falls in the 4--10 ms range, the scheduler assigns float16 compression and $\rho=2$ (synchronisation every two local epochs).


\subsection{Compression Strategies}
We apply activation/gradient compression to both smashed activations and cut layer gradients using four communication modes: float32, float16, int8, and top-k (implemented as top-k mode). Compression is evaluated in both static and adaptive settings: in static settings, one mode is fixed for the whole run, while in adaptive settings, the scheduler dynamically switches modes according to latency conditions. This design reduces payload size while allowing the system to balance communication efficiency and training stability under varying network conditions.

\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Data Acquisition and Preprocessing}
\label{subsec:dataset}
To validate the effectiveness of the proposed system, we conducted a case study using real-world meteorological observations from Newcastle upon Tyne, UK. We selected 12 sensors across distinct geographic coordinates as data sources, with the raw data retrieved via the Open Meteo Historical Weather API  which is powered by high-resolution reanalysis models such as ERA5 \cite{hersbach2020era5}.

Within this network architecture, these 12 coordinates are defined as sensing nodes, each extracting five key features: temperature, humidity, surface pressure, wind speed, and precipitation. Following feature selection conventions in ERA5 \cite{hersbach2020era5} and WeatherBench \cite{rasp2020weatherbench}, these features are selected to cover both dynamic drivers and historical patterns of rainfall. Temperature and humidity indicate moisture capacity, while surface pressure and wind speed characterise atmospheric motion; historical precipitation helps the model capture temporal autocorrelation \cite{saeed2021rainfall}. Together, these five features provide a multidimensional weather profile that enables the FSL system to distinguish short showers from sustained frontal rainfall.

To simulate a realistic federated setting, we implemented a non-IID partition strategy. The 12 sensing nodes were divided into three non-overlapping subsets and assigned to three individual clients. Each client manages data from four geographically distinct nodes, with each node's dataset comprising the aforementioned five key features.

For temporal modelling, we use a 24-hour sliding window to capture preceding meteorological context and predict cumulative precipitation in the subsequent 3-hour window. The dataset is split chronologically to preserve temporal integrity: the final 14 days are used as the test set, and the preceding 14 days are used as the validation set. This split avoids temporal leakage and supports fair evaluation.

As shown in Table \ref{tab:dataset_stats}, the dataset exhibits significant sparsity, with measurable precipitation ($>0$\,mm) accounting for only $20.8\%$ of total observations. For model training and evaluation, we apply a $0.1$\,mm detection threshold to define a ``rainy event,'' aligning with the World Meteorological Organization standard for minimum measurable precipitation to filter out sensor noise \cite{wmo2018guide}. This distribution creates a significant class imbalance, where "non-rainy" observations ($79.2\%$) vastly outnumber "rainy" events ($20.8\%$). Due to this class imbalance, our evaluation prioritises Recall and F1 score for event detection, complemented by Mean Squared Error (MSE) and Mean Absolute Error (MAE) for assessing regression accuracy.

\subsection{Multitask Training and Configuration}
\label{subsec:training}
The multitask learning framework described in Section \ref{sec:Methodology} is optimised using a consistent hyperparameter suite to ensure that performance variations across scenarios are attributable solely to communication strategies. We employ the Adam optimiser with a learning rate of $0.0005$ and a batch size of 32. The training process is executed for 30 global rounds, with each client performing 10 local iterations per round before a synchronisation decision is triggered.

To normalise the high variance in rainfall intensity, the regression target is processed using a $\log(1+x)$ transformation. This transformation smooths the data by shrinking the numerical gap between light and heavy rainfall, which prevents extreme weather events from excessively influencing the model. The joint objective function is weighted to prioritise rain event detection, assigning a coefficient of $2.0$ to the Focal Loss ($\gamma=2.0$, $\alpha=0.25$) and $1.0$ to the regression loss. During inference, we apply a classification guided gating rule with a probability threshold of $0.34$; rainfall intensity is only predicted when the classifier identifies a high probability event, thereby reducing false alarms in dry environments.

\subsection{System Configuration and Adaptive Control Settings}
\label{subsec:deployment}
The platform is implemented as a containerised environment using Docker and gRPC to simulate the orchestration and communication overhead of a distributed edge network. The experiments were executed on a standard Laptop with NVIDIA RTX 4090 GPU and 64GB of RAM to maintain sufficient compute throughput for parallel execution of all nodes.

In alignment with the adaptive communication control strategies discussed in Section \ref{sec:RelatedWork}, our proposed scheduler is initialised with empirical control settings derived from our configuration system. It is important to note that all parameters defined below serve as the initial state; the system dynamically escalates system level optimisations during runtime based on EMA smoothed latency:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Network Smoothing}: Latency estimation utilises an Exponential Moving Average (EMA) with a smoothing factor $\alpha_{ema} = 0.2$ to mitigate stochastic RTT noise.
    \item \textbf{Hierarchical Compression Thresholds}: Communication oriented optimisation is triggered at three levels: \textbf{Float16} at 4.0ms, \textbf{INT8} at 10.0ms, and \textbf{Top-k} (density 0.5) at 15.0ms (calculated as 1.5$\times$ the INT8 threshold).
    \item \textbf{Dynamic Synchronisation Interval}: The synchronisation interval $\rho$ follows a stepwise growth logic. As network severity increases, $\rho$ scales from its base value up to a maximum safety limit of 20, reducing synchronisation frequency to preserve local compute autonomy and reduce cumulative communication costs.
\end{itemize}

\section{Results and Analysis}
\label{sec:Res}
This section evaluates the proposed communication efficient FSL framework using the experiment matrix described in Section \ref{subsec:matrix}. We report results over 10 random seeds and summarise each metric as mean $\pm$ standard deviation to reflect both performance and stability. Since rainfall events are relatively sparse, Macro F1 is treated as the primary classification metric, while Micro F1 and Accuracy are reported as complementary indicators. In addition, we analyse communication and system behaviour using average payload size, reported latency, training duration, and server aggregation rounds.

The analysis is organised by research questions: (RQ1) overall performance versus baseline, (RQ2) the effect of compression strategies, (RQ3) the impact of synchronisation interval $\rho$, (RQ4) adaptive behaviour under medium, burst, and extreme latency profiles, and (RQ5) the final accuracy--efficiency trade-off. Unless otherwise stated, comparisons are made within matched latency settings to isolate the effect of each design component.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:Discussion}
\subsection{Imbalanced Task}
Rainfall prediction is inherently affected by class imbalance, as non rainy periods dominate while rainfall events are relatively rare. In this study, our goal is not to maximise absolute predictive accuracy, but to isolate the computation communication performance trade-off under a fixed model backbone and training protocol. Therefore, imbalance handling components (focal/class weighting, thresholding, and rain aware oversampling) are intentionally kept fixed across scenarios to ensure fair comparison of communication policies. This makes event detection challenging, especially in recall oriented settings where missing rare events is costly. The current results should therefore be interpreted as a controlled baseline for system level analysis, and future work can further improve predictive quality through dedicated model and data level optimisation.

\subsection{Scheduler}
The proposed scheduler is rule-based and relies on profiler generated latency signals. Although this design is simple, interpretable, and practical, it is inherently limited in flexibility. The mapping between latency conditions and control responses is manually specified, which restricts adaptation to unseen or highly dynamic environments. In practice, the interaction among latency, communication overhead, computation overhead, and convergence is often nonlinear. More advanced approaches, such as reinforcement learning or control theoretic schedulers, may provide more data-driven policy decisions.

\subsection{Small scale Deployment}
Compared with real distributed systems, the current setup is relatively small-scale. Although multiple random seeds are used, the platform includes only three clients and is fully implemented in a Docker based simulation environment. This supports controlled experiments but does not fully capture real world heterogeneity. In particular, because experiments are not deployed on physical edge devices (e.g., Raspberry Pi), factors such as constrained compute, thermal throttling, and unstable wireless links are not modelled.

In addition, all experiments are executed on a standard laptop where resources are shared with background processes. This can introduce variability in runtime measurements due to resource contention. Therefore, the results should be interpreted as proof of concept rather than full real world validation.
\subsection{No Client Dropout}
The experiments assume stable participation without client dropout, packet loss, or communication interruptions. However, such events are common in real IoT systems because of unstable networks and device constraints. Since Federated Split Learning relies on both activation exchange and synchronisation, it can be sensitive to unreliable communication. As a result, the current evaluation may overestimate robustness. Future work should include partial participation, delayed updates, and communication failures.
\subsection{Dataset Location Specificity}
Finally, the dataset used in this study comes from the Newcastle area, which means the learned patterns are location specific. Climate conditions, seasonal behaviour, and weather dynamics can vary substantially across regions. Therefore, the findings may not transfer directly to other cities. Evaluating the framework on multiple geographic regions is necessary to verify robustness and generalisability.

\section{Conclusion}
This paper introduces a communication efficient Federated Split Learning (FSL) framework for rainfall prediction in distributed IoT environments. By combining spatial compression techniques (quantisation and Top-k sparsification) with temporal synchronisation control, the proposed system addresses the dual communication bottlenecks of payload size and synchronisation frequency. At the same time, reduced server rounds and runtime improvements in selected settings indicate practical system level computation benefits.

In experiments across 14 scenarios (10 random seeds each), results show a consistent trade-off between accuracy and efficiency rather than a single dominant strategy. First, compression strategies such as INT8 quantisation reduce payload by up to 73.4\% (256.0 bytes in M05 to 68.0 bytes in M06), with an average Macro F1 decrease of about 0.038. Second, increasing the synchronisation interval (from $\rho=1$ in M05 to $\rho=3$ in M08) reduces server rounds from 30 to 10 and shortens training time by 54.6\% (318.6s to 143.1s), with a moderate Macro F1 decrease of 0.016. Third, adaptive scheduling is particularly useful under harsher latency conditions for payload reduction (e.g., M12 shows a 20.7\% payload reduction with near neutral Macro F1 change), while runtime gains remain scenario dependent (e.g., M14 is 63.4\% faster than M13 with a 0.022 Macro F1 drop). Overall, pairwise comparisons indicate that compression mainly improves payload efficiency, larger $\rho$ primarily improves runtime and aggregation workload, and adaptation improves robustness to latency variation.

Overall, the results validate the framework's potential for practical IoT applications, where communication constraints are paramount. The framework maintains acceptable Macro F1 scores (ranging from 0.429 to 0.478 across scenarios) while significantly reducing communication overhead. Future research directions include exploring reinforcement learning based schedulers for more adaptive policy selection, larger scale deployments with physical edge devices to capture real world heterogeneity, and robustness enhancements against client dropout and packet loss to further advance communication efficient distributed learning in real world settings.
