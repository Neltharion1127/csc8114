% ---- Abstract ----
\begin{abstract}
  Federated Split Learning (FSL) has emerged as a promising paradigm for privacy-preserving distributed model training on resource-constrained IoT devices. However, the frequent transmission of intermediate activations between clients and the central server introduces substantial communication overhead that existing methods do not adequately address. In particular, both the size of each transmitted activation and the synchronization frequency are rarely treated as independent, controllable variables within a unified system. In this paper, we propose a lightweight communication optimization system for FSL-based rainfall prediction using data from the Newcastle Urban Observatory. The system incorporates two complementary mechanisms: (i) an Activation Compression Module that applies quantization (float32 to float16 or int8) and Top-$k$ sparsification to reduce per-transmission payload size, and (ii) a Synchronization Manager that controls the global aggregation interval $\rho$ to limit the total number of communication rounds. A lightweight Client Profiler records per-round latency and upload volume, which a rule-based Scheduler uses to adapt compression and synchronization configurations per client. Experiments evaluate the trade-off between communication cost and model accuracy across varying compression levels and synchronization intervals, providing practical guidelines for deploying FSL in real-world IoT sensing environments.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
Rainfall prediction has become increasingly important for urban resilience and smart water management systems, particularly with the proliferation of IoT sensors and distributed environmental monitoring. While recent studies have achieved notable improvements in predictive modelling accuracy, many of them overlook system-level constraints such as communication cost, scalability, and deployment feasibility in resource-constrained environments \cite{10.3389/frwa.2024.1378598}. This limitation becomes more critical when rainfall forecasting models are deployed across edge devices connected via wireless networks. 

Federated Split Learning (FSL) has been proposed to reduce computational burden on edge devices by splitting model training between clients and a central server. By allowing early layers to be trained locally and later layers centrally, FSL reduces client-side computation while preserving data privacy. However, communication cost and scalability remain significant challenges. Frequent transmission of smashed activations and gradients leads to high communication overhead, and maintaining server-side states for multiple clients may limit scalability. Recent works attempt to mitigate these issues through dynamic split points, gradient aggregation, asynchronous training, or selective communication strategies \cite{10478872}. Asynchronous training mechanisms reduce communication bottlenecks by relaxing strict client–server synchronization constraints \cite{2021arXiv210709786C}. While these methods improve efficiency under certain conditions, they often increase architectural complexity, rely on centralized coordination, or assume relatively stable network conditions, limiting their adaptability in dynamic wireless environments. 

A related research direction focuses on communication-efficient federated learning through parameter compression. For example, compressive sensing–based approaches reduce bidirectional communication via adaptive compression ratios \cite{10078005}. Similarly, communication-efficient personalized federated edge learning frameworks employ gradient compression to mitigate bandwidth pressure in wireless scenarios \cite{2024ITWC...23.7362C}. These approaches effectively reduce the size of transmitted model updates. However, their optimization primarily targets gradients or weights in standard FL architectures. In Split FL settings, smashed activations transmitted between client and server can dominate communication overhead. Moreover, compression methods often rely on sparsity assumptions that may deteriorate as training progresses, potentially degrading reconstruction quality and model performance. Importantly, although transmission size is reduced, synchronization frequency is typically unchanged, meaning that communication events remain frequent. 

Other works attempt to reduce computation or storage overhead through model pruning and lightweight architectures. Adaptive pruning strategies adjust model complexity based on heterogeneous device capabilities, improving feasibility in resource-constrained environments \cite{10050151}. Lightweight rainfall prediction models also aim to balance forecasting accuracy and computational efficiency \cite{10.3389/frwa.2024.1378598}. Nevertheless, these improvements remain largely structural. Clients usually continue to participate in each synchronization round, leaving the temporal communication pattern intact. As a result, cumulative communication cost may still be substantial even if model size is reduced. 

Across these categories, a consistent limitation emerges. Communication efficiency is typically addressed at a single layer—parameter compression \cite{10078005}, model pruning \cite{10050151}, architectural redesign in split learning \cite{2023arXiv230205599M,2026ITWC...25.1981L,10522472}, or application-specific lightweight modelling \cite{CHAN2023100375,10.3389/frwa.2024.1378598}. However, few studies systematically analyze communication behaviour within the Split FL interaction loop itself. In practical deployments, smashed activations may constitute a substantial portion of transmitted data, and synchronization frequency directly influences both bandwidth consumption and convergence stability. Yet the joint impact of activation representation and synchronization interval on communication–convergence trade-offs remains insufficiently quantified. 

This gap suggests that beyond structural or architectural modifications, there is a need to examine lightweight and adaptive mechanisms that directly control (i) how activations are represented during transmission and (ii) how often synchronization occurs between client and server. A systematic evaluation of these two factors under heterogeneous wireless conditions can provide clearer insight into the trade-offs between communication cost, accuracy degradation, and convergence behaviour, thereby informing more scalable and practical Split Federated Learning systems. 

 

Rainfall prediction in urban environments has increasingly relied on IoT-based sensing networks, making communication efficiency and model deployability as critical as predictive accuracy. While numerous studies have demonstrated the effectiveness of machine learning and deep learning models for rainfall forecasting \cite{10.3389/frwa.2024.1378598}, recent literature reveals an imbalance: research overwhelmingly prioritizes accuracy while overlooking the system-level constraints in real urban drainage and climate resilience contexts \cite{CHAN2023100375}. To organize existing work and highlight gaps, current approaches can be broadly classified into three categories: (1) communication-efficient learning frameworks, (2) computation- or model-structure–oriented optimization, and (3) Split or Federated-Split Learning architectures. 

First, communication-efficient Federated Learning (FL) largely focuses on gradient or parameter compression to reduce transmission cost. Compressive sensing–based methods dynamically adjust compression ratios to reduce bidirectional communication \cite{10078005}, while gradient quantization and sparsification frameworks address wireless bandwidth restrictions in edge learning scenarios \cite{2024ITWC...23.7362C}. These methods demonstrate substantial communication savings, yet they almost exclusively target weight or gradient updates in traditional FL. In Federated Split Learning (FSL), however, the majority of communication arises not from gradients but from the exchange of smashed activations between client and server. Consequently, compression strategies designed for FL become misaligned with the communication patterns of SL, where activation size grows with network depth. More critically, activation compression often relies on sparsity assumptions that degrade as training progresses, reducing reconstruction quality. This reveals a fundamental limitation: communication-efficient FL techniques are often insufficient for the unique requirements of FSL. 

Second, computation-oriented approaches—such as model pruning, adaptive layer reduction, or lightweight network design—aim to reduce computation on resource-constrained clients. Adaptive pruning strategies adjust model structure based on device capability \cite{10050151}, while lightweight rainfall prediction models balance accuracy and efficiency in urban contexts \cite{10.3389/frwa.2024.1378598}. However, these improvements remain primarily structural; they do not alter the temporal communication patterns of FL or FSL. Clients must still participate in every synchronisation round, meaning the cumulative communication volume remains high despite reductions in model size. In IoT-based rainfall monitoring, where devices are energy-limited and rely on unstable wireless networks, structural optimizations alone fail to address the crucial need to jointly optimize representation precision, activation size, and communication frequency. 

Third, Split Learning (SL) and Federated-Split Learning (FSL) were introduced to distribute computation more flexibly between clients and servers. By training early layers locally and later layers centrally, FSL reduces client-side computational burden while preserving privacy \cite{2023arXiv230205599M}. Yet, communication cost and scalability remain persistent bottlenecks. The transmission of activations in every forward pass and gradients at every backward pass produces high communication loads, especially in multi-client IoT deployments. Although more recent works propose dynamic split point selection \cite{2023arXiv230205599M}, asynchronous training and relaxed synchronisation \cite{2021arXiv210709786C}, architectural redesign to reduce server-state dependencies \cite{2026ITWC...25.1981L,10522472}, or proactive stop policies and gradient aggregation to alleviate bandwidth pressure \cite{10478872}, these strategies often introduce additional complexity or assume relatively stable connectivity—conditions rarely met in real-world environmental sensing systems. 

Across all three research categories, a common limitation becomes clear: existing approaches typically optimize only one dimension—whether compression \cite{10078005}, pruning \cite{10050151}, split architecture design \cite{2023arXiv230205599M,2026ITWC...25.1981L}, or domain-specific modelling and rainfall projection studies \cite{CHAN2023100375,10.3389/frwa.2024.1378598}—without addressing the interdependencies between data representation precision, activation size, and synchronisation frequency. Moreover, despite the rapid deployment of IoT environmental sensing platforms, few studies evaluate their methods under real-world constraints such as those present in Newcastle’s Urban Observatory (UO), where heterogeneous sensors and unstable connectivity dominate system behaviour. 

In summary, prior research provides valuable advancements in either communication or computation efficiency but lacks a unified, lightweight, and tunable mechanism specifically tailored for Federated Split Learning in resource-constrained IoT environments. Motivated by these gaps, this study aims to develop a communication optimization mechanism that jointly considers data representation precision and synchronisation frequency, enabling scalable and practical deployment of FSL for rainfall prediction in real-world sensing networks. 


\section{Proposed System}
\label{sec:system}

While existing literature extensively explores gradient compression in traditional FL, the communication overhead of ``smashed activations'' in Federated Split Learning (FSL) remains under-investigated. Furthermore, synchronization frequency ($\rho$) is often treated as a fixed or coupled parameter rather than an independent controllable variable. This project aims to bridge this gap by proposing a lightweight system that optimizes communication in both precision and frequency dimensions.

\subsection{System Design}

This project proposes a lightweight communication optimization system for Federated Split Learning (FSL) that systematically investigates the synergy between activation compression and reduced synchronization frequency, with the goal of improving communication efficiency while preserving convergence stability and inherent privacy guarantees.

\subsubsection{Activation Compression (Spatial Optimization)}

We reduce transmission volume by lowering the representation precision of smashed activations:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Quantization}: float32 $\rightarrow$ float16 or int8
  \item \textbf{Top-$k$ Sparsification}: transmitting only the $k$ most significant activation values, discarding near-zero entries
\end{itemize}
This component evaluates the trade-off between communication cost and predictive accuracy under different precision levels.

\subsubsection{Reduced Synchronization Frequency (Temporal Optimization)}

We introduce a tunable parameter $\rho$ to control synchronization intervals between clients and the server. Instead of synchronizing every batch, communication occurs once every $N$ batches. This mechanism treats synchronization frequency as an independent variable and examines its effect on convergence speed, stability, and predictive performance.

\subsection{Research Questions}

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{RQ1 (Accuracy--Communication Trade-off)}: How does activation quantization (e.g., float32 $\rightarrow$ int8) impact predictive accuracy, and is there an ``optimal range'' that balances data volume with model performance?
  \item \textbf{RQ2 (Convergence Behavior)}: How does reducing synchronization frequency ($\rho$) affect the convergence speed and training stability of the Split Learning model?
  \item \textbf{RQ3 (Joint Optimization Effect)}: When spatial compression and temporal decoupling are combined, does a synergistic effect emerge that further enhances overall system throughput?
\end{itemize} 


 




\section{High-level design}
\label{sec:design}

\subsection{System Overview}

We design a communication-efficient Federated Split Learning (FSL) architecture for distributed rainfall prediction, specifically using environmental data from the Newcastle Urban Observatory (UO). The system consists of multiple client nodes (edge devices) and one central server, as illustrated in Fig.~\ref{fig:arch}. Each client holds a local portion of the rainfall dataset and performs partial model training.

The neural network is split at a predefined cut layer into a client-side sub-network and a server-side sub-network. Edge devices handle only the earlier layers, reducing their computational burden, while the server handles the rest. During training, clients perform a forward pass up to the cut layer, transmit the intermediate activation to the server, and later receive the corresponding gradient to update their local parameters.

The main goal of this design is to reduce communication overhead by controlling two independent variables: (i) the size of each transmitted activation, via compression, and (ii) how often clients synchronize with the server, via a fixed synchronization interval $\rho$.

\subsection{Client-side Modules}

Each client has three main components: a local sub-model with its optimizer, an \textit{Activation Compression Module}, and a \textit{Client Profiler}.

\subsubsection{Activation Compression Module}

Before sending the intermediate activation to the server, this module reduces the data size to lower bandwidth usage. Two strategies are supported:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Quantization}: converting activations from float32 to float16 or int8, reducing payload size by $2\times$ to $4\times$.
  \item \textbf{Top-$k$ Sparsification}: transmitting only the $k$ most significant activation values and their indices, dropping near-zero entries.
\end{itemize}
The server decompresses or reconstructs the received activation before continuing forward propagation. The compression strategy is assigned by the server-side Scheduler at the start of each round.

\subsubsection{Client Profiler}

The Client Profiler is a lightweight logging component that records two key metrics at the end of each training round: the total round latency ($t_{\mathrm{round}}$) and the number of bytes uploaded. These values are sent to the server along with the activation, adding negligible overhead. The server uses them as input to the Scheduler.

\subsection{Server-side Modules}
\label{sec:server_modules}

The server hosts four modules: a \textit{Coordinator}, a \textit{Scheduler}, a \textit{Synchronization Manager}, and a \textit{Metrics Collector}.

\subsubsection{Coordinator / Aggregator}

The Coordinator manages the distributed training loop. It receives compressed activations from clients, performs server-side forward and backward propagation, and returns cut-layer gradients. Periodically, it collects the local model weights from all clients and applies Federated Averaging (FedAvg) to update the global model. The server only processes compressed intermediate data, so raw client data never leaves the client device.

\subsubsection{Scheduler}

The Scheduler reads the profiler data reported by each client and adjusts their configurations for the next round using a simple rule-based policy. For example, if a client's round latency exceeds a threshold (e.g., $t_{\mathrm{round}} > 1\,\mathrm{s}$), the Scheduler assigns it a more aggressive compression level (e.g., int8) or a larger synchronization interval $\rho$ to reduce its communication load. Clients performing within normal latency bounds are kept at the default settings. This approach is straightforward to implement and easy to reason about.

\subsubsection{Synchronization Manager}

This module controls how often the global model is updated. The synchronization interval $\rho$ assigned by the Scheduler determines how many local training steps a client performs before the next global aggregation. A larger $\rho$ means fewer communication rounds per epoch, which reduces total data transferred but may affect convergence stability.

\subsubsection{Metrics Collector}

The Metrics Collector aggregates system-wide performance indicators for monitoring and post-hoc evaluation. The following metrics are recorded throughout training:

\begin{itemize}
  \item Total transmitted bytes (uplink and downlink)
  \item Per-round latency ($t_{\mathrm{round}}$ per client)
  \item Per-client bytes uploaded ($b_{\mathrm{up}}$)
  \item Global model accuracy and loss at each aggregation step
\end{itemize}

These measurements form the basis for evaluating the communication--accuracy trade-offs investigated in this work.

\subsection{Training and Communication Workflow}

As illustrated in Fig.~\ref{fig:seq}, the complete training workflow for each communication round proceeds as follows:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item Each selected client performs forward propagation on its local sub-model up to the cut layer.
  \item The Activation Compression Module compresses the resulting activation.
  \item The client transmits the compressed activation to the server.
  \item The server decompresses the activation, completes forward propagation, evaluates the loss, and performs backward propagation on the server-side sub-model.
  \item The server sends cut-layer gradients back to the corresponding client.
  \item The client completes local backpropagation and updates its parameters.
  \item The server's Scheduler reads the profiler data, and if any client's latency exceeds the threshold, it adjusts that client's compression level or $\rho$ for the next round.
\end{enumerate}

The synchronization interval $\rho$ is either fixed globally or assigned per-client by the Scheduler. Setting $\rho = 1$ corresponds to synchronizing after every batch; increasing $\rho$ reduces the number of communication rounds, which saves bandwidth but may affect convergence.

\subsection{Design Rationale}

The design favours simplicity and transparency. The Scheduler uses a rule-based threshold policy rather than a learned model, which makes the system easy to implement, test, and explain. Both the compression level and the synchronization interval are explicit, observable parameters, so we can run controlled experiments and directly measure the impact of each on accuracy and communication cost.

Together, the Activation Compression Module (spatial) and the Synchronization Manager (temporal), guided by the Scheduler, address our research questions: how much accuracy is lost from lower-precision activations (RQ1), how convergence is affected by less frequent synchronization (RQ2), and whether combining both yields an efficient overall system (RQ3).
